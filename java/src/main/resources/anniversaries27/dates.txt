In Soviet Russia,  mainframe computers Ural-4 and MINSK-2  were  developed. 

Ural-4 was based on vacuum tubes (valves), with the hardware being able to perform 12,000 floating-point calculations per second. Memory was not based on 8-bit bytes, but on 40- bit words. One word was able to contain either one numeric value or two instructions. Ferrite core was used as operative memory.

The MINSK-2 was much more modern. It was a transistor-based digital computer.

1952: (75 y.a.)
On May 21, 1952 IBM company released the IBM 701,which was first commercial, scientific computer in history.

BESM (БЭСМ) was the series of Soviet mainframe computers built in 1950–60s. BESM-1, originally referred to as simply the BESM or BESM AN ("BESM Akademii Nauk", BESM of the Academy of Sciences), was completed in 1952. Only one such  machine was built. The machine used approximately 5,000 vacuum tubes. At the time of completion, it was the fastest computer in Europe.
